# Fare

I had not been home in four months.

Home was Pune — a flat where my mother kept my room exactly as I had left it, the books on the shelf and the notes in the drawer all belonging to a version of myself that no longer existed but which she maintained with the quiet devotion of a woman tending a garden for someone who may never return to walk through it. My younger sister called few days ago, and the way they said *"when are you coming"* was never really a question. It was a sentence passed in the tone of a question. A conviction. An indictment of distance.

I had been living in Udupi, a small coastal town six hours southwest of Bengaluru, in a large rented house with two friends. We cooked our own food. We were trying to build things — a company, a product, some version of a life that didn't require us to sit under fluorescent lights in a corporate office and slowly forget that we had once been capable of wanting something. Udupi had given me what I didn't know I needed: salt air, silence, cheap fish from the morning market, and the strange permission that comes with living in a place where nobody cares about your LinkedIn profile. But silence, I was learning, makes the people who love you hear your absence more loudly. Every week the calls from Pune grew shorter in words and longer in meaning.

Friday night, I had promised. Clearly. Unambiguously. *"I'll take the bus tonight."*

I did not take the bus.

I was in Bengaluru for some work, staying at a college friend's flat in the eastern sprawl of the city — that zone of glass towers and construction dust where India's technology industry has built its Vatican. Saturday morning my mother called. Not angry. Never angry. Something more surgical than anger — *patience*. The patience of a woman who has been promised things by the men in her life enough times to have developed a complete epistemological framework for evaluating the reliability of my speech.

"You said Friday night. It is Saturday. You are not here."

"I'll fly," I said. "Today itself. Morning flight."

I booked a 6:30 AM flight to Pune. The airport was about forty kilometres north of where I was staying. At four in the morning, with empty roads, that is a forty-minute drive. I set my alarm. I had a plan. It was a good plan. It had the singular flaw of all good plans, which is that it was set in Bengaluru.

---

Let me say something about this city, for those who have never been.

Bengaluru is India's answer to the question *"What if Silicon Valley had ten million people and was built on roads designed for bullock carts?"* It is where a quarter of the country's software engineers live, where the world's back offices hum through the night, where twenty-three-year-olds earn more money than their fathers ever dreamed of and then spend two hours getting home in traffic so dense it has its own weather system. It is a city of profound, almost metaphysical contradiction — a place that writes code for companies in San Francisco and Tokyo but cannot, for reasons that may be spiritual, build a road that lasts through one monsoon. Cranes hang over every major street like steel birds frozen in mid-flight. A metro system is being built, and has been being built, for so long that it has become less a transportation project than a generational saga, a kind of civil engineering *Mahabharata* whose completion, like the heat death of the universe, is theoretically certain but practically beyond the horizon of any living person's experience.

And in the early morning hours — before the traffic, before the chaos, before ten million ambitions collide on six-lane roads reduced to two by construction barriers — there exists a brief window of mercy. The air turns cool. The roads open up. For forty minutes, if you catch it right, the city almost works.

I needed those forty minutes.

---

I woke at four. My friend was asleep, face-down, in a position that suggested not rest but surrender — as if consciousness had not faded but fled. He had offered to drive me. His car keys were on the kitchen counter. His soul was in another timezone. I let him sleep. I booked a cab on my phone at 4:20, sat on the bed, and watched the screen.

The blue dot of my driver was doing something I can only describe as abstract. It moved laterally. It stopped. It retraced itself. It moved again in a direction that was neither toward me nor away from me but perpendicular to all reason, as though the man behind the wheel were navigating not by road but by dream logic. I had texted him when I booked — *" Airport ride, I have a flight at 6:30, please come as early as you can"* — with the particular faith of man who has lived in India long enough to know that such messages function less as communication and more as prayer.

By 4:35, my friend — perhaps stirred by some residual guilt, or by the light from my phone — appeared in the hallway, squinting, wearing the expression of a man who has been woken by his own conscience and resents it.

"The cab's coming," I said. "Go back to sleep."

"I'll come down."

He walked me downstairs to the gate of his apartment complex, barefoot, arms crossed against the early March air. We stood together in the grey quiet, two twenty-three-year-olds in that strange season of life when you are old enough to have a salary and young enough to have no idea what you are doing with it. He yawned. I refreshed the app.

"He's close," I said.

"You'll make it," he said.

He said this with the confidence of one who did not need to make it and therefore could afford to be optimistic about another one's timeline.

The headlights appeared at 4:40.

---

A white sedan — the ubiquitous, egg-shaped hatchback that serves as the unofficial vehicle of India's gig economy, produced in such quantities that I am convinced they are not manufactured but *spawned* — pulled up to the gate. I clasped my friend's hand, threw my bag in the back seat, and sat down.

I knew within minutes that I was in trouble.

It was the eyes. They were open, technically. They met mine in the rearview mirror. But they had the quality of lights left on in a house whose occupants have gone on vacation — burning, yes, but illuminating nothing, serving no one. The driver was a young man, late twenties, lean, with the hollowed-out look of a person who has been awake so long his body has stopped protesting and simply begun to metabolise itself. He was from Hoskote — a small, dusty town on the city's eastern edge where Bengaluru's ambitions finally exhaust themselves and dissolve into the older, slower India of fields and lorries and highway tea stalls.

"Airport, sir?"

"Yes. 6:30 flight. We should hurry."

He nodded. It was a good nod. Firm. Decisive. Completely unconnected to anything that followed.

He pulled out of the gate, and from the car's speakers — at a volume that I can only describe as *medicinal* — there erupted a song. A thumping, bass-heavy, synthesiser-drenched anthem of the kind produced in the film studios of South India, in which a woman invites a man to do things that would be illegal in several jurisdictions, over a beat designed less for dancing than for the resuscitation of the clinically drowsy. The car shook with it. My molars vibrated. I understood immediately what was happening: this was not entertainment. This was a man using pop music the way a field medic uses adrenaline — jabbed directly into the nervous system to keep the organism operational for one more hour, one more fare, one more ride to the airport.

I checked the app. **ETA: 5:20 AM.** That gave me seventy minutes before my flight. Tight. Not comfortable. But survivable.

I did not yet understand that this number, like most numbers in this city, was a work of fiction.

---

The first turn he missed was subtle. A left that would have put us on the expressway — the wide, relatively empty corridor that cuts north toward the airport. I saw it slide past us the way you see a word slide past you in a conversation when you are too slow to say it and the moment is gone. I opened my mouth. We were already past it.

"That was the —"

"Ohh. I know another way."

He did not know another way.

What followed was a tour of eastern Bengaluru's most obscure back roads — narrow, unlit, potholed stretches where stray dogs trotted with the relaxed confidence of creatures who understood that they were the apex predators of this ecosystem. We bumped through half-built layouts where apartment complexes stood in various stages of completion like monuments to interrupted ambition. At one point we drove through what I believe was an unpaved service road behind a warehouse, though I cannot be certain because the only light was the blue glow of the navigation app on his phone, which was itself recalculating with the quiet panic of a machine that has lost faith in its own algorithms.

Then he stopped.

Not slowed. *Stopped.* Pulled over to the shoulder of whatever road we were on — I had lost geographic certainty — got out of the car, walked to a small shop that was, against all logic, open at five in the morning, and bought a packet of cigarettes and a small bottle of face wash. He returned to the car. He squeezed the face wash into his palms. He rubbed it over his face with the slow, circular motions of a man performing a ritual ablution. Water dripped from his chin onto his shirt. He lit a cigarette. He took two long drags. He put the car in gear.

I stared at him.

"Bro," I said, and my voice had the careful, diplomatic tenor of a man who is trying very hard not to shout, "I have a flight."

"Yes, sir. Going only."

"Why did you accept this ride if you were this sleepy?"

He looked at me in the rearview mirror, and for the first time his eyes had something behind them — not alertness, but something older. Amusement, maybe. Or the exhaustion that has gone so deep it resembles wisdom.

"Sir, if I don't accept, someone else will. Then what? I go home with nothing?"

I didn't answer. He unwrapped a candy — one of those small, sweet, chemical-orange toffees that cost half a rupee — and put it in his mouth. He turned the music up. A new song. Kannada this time. Another woman. Another invitation. The bass resumed its work on my skeleton. We drove.

---

It was somewhere on that dark stretch of road — or perhaps it was already the highway, I had lost the ability to distinguish — that we began to talk. Not because either of us wanted to, but because silence at five in the morning between two strangers in a car moving at sixty kilometres an hour through the outskirts of a city that is trying to become the future has a weight that eventually forces someone to speak.

He asked what I did.

"Engineer," I said.

He looked at the road ahead — at the cranes and barriers and torn-up asphalt where the metro construction had turned a four-lane highway into an obstacle course — and nodded.

"You help build all this? Roads and metro?"

"No. Software. Computers."

"Oh." A pause. "Software." Another pause. Then, with the frank, guiltless curiosity of a man who has no reason to be delicate: "Did you get laid off?"

I laughed. It came out before I could shape it into anything polished or defensive. Just a laugh — short, honest, surprised.

"No. Not yet."

"Good," he said, without conviction, unwrapping another candy. "Many have."

And then he told me. In the way that people tell you things at five in the morning — freely, without structure, the way water moves downhill. He told me that in the last year, something had changed on the roads of this city. The cabs had multiplied. Not because the city had grown — it was always growing — but because the people driving them had changed. They were engineers now. Software developers. Men and women who had once sat in the glass towers we were driving past, who had written code and attended meetings and received salaries that would have made their parents weep with pride, and who had then, one Tuesday or Thursday, received an email or a calendar invite to a conference room with an HR manager, and walked out carrying a cardboard box and a severance package and the particular expression of a person who has just learned that the future they were promised was a contract, and contracts end.

"They drive part-time first," he said. "Morning-evening, before office, after office. Uber, Rapido, bike taxi. Then some —" he made a gesture that meant *fully*, completely, with both hands and no net — "full-time."

He said it without judgment. Perhaps even with a trace of something like respect. These were people who had been expelled from one economy and, instead of dying, had walked into another. They had downloaded an app, turned their commute into a commodity, and started selling the only thing the city had left them — their willingness to move through it.

"Some are starting tea stalls also," he said, brightening slightly, the way a man brightens when the conversation arrives at his own territory. "Near Hoskote toll. I also started one. Seeing them only I got the idea. It is doing well."

He said *doing well* the way a farmer says *the rain came* — with gratitude that does not fully trust its own luck.

"Engineers are starting tea stalls?" I asked.

"Sir, what to do. Rent is there. EMI is there. Family is there. You think they want to sell tea? They want to write software only. But software doesn't want them."

He said this — *software doesn't want them* — with the clean, accidental poetry of a man who has never read a book about economics but has understood, through the daily practice of being alive in this city, something that most books about economics take three hundred pages to say. That industries are not loyal. That cities are not grateful. That the same machine that lifts you can, with no malice and no memory, set you down.

I looked out the window. We were passing a stretch of road where the metro pillars rose from the earth like the bones of some enormous buried animal being slowly exhumed. Between them, in the pale pre-dawn light, I could see the labour camps — rows of tin-roofed sheds where the construction workers slept, men who had come from villages in the north and east to build a transit system they would never ride, in a city they would never belong to, for wages that would be sent home to families they saw once a year if the trains were running and the contractor paid on time.

And above them, the glass towers. Dark now, but in a few hours they would light up, and inside them, men and women my age would sit before screens and build products for people in countries they had never visited, solving problems they had never personally experienced, moving numbers that would become other numbers that would eventually, through a series of abstractions so long that no single human could trace the chain, become the salary that became the rent that became the reason someone else was driving a cab at five in the morning to make sure the chain didn't break.

I looked at my phone. **ETA: 6:10 AM.**

My stomach dropped.

---

I called the airline. A woman answered with the professionally warm voice of someone trained to deliver bad news as though it were a weather report.

"Sir, check-in closes thirty minutes before departure. After 6 AM, we most likely will not be able to board you."

"The flight is at 6:30. I will be there by 6:10."

"Sir, I understand. But most likely it will not be possible."

*Most likely it will not be possible.* I have never heard a sentence that so perfectly captures the relationship between a man and his plans. I hung up. I looked at the guy. He was unwrapping another candy. The music was still playing. A Tamil song now — a man was making promises to a woman with the same sincerity and the same probability of fulfilment as my Uber's original ETA.

"Brother," I said, and this time I did not modulate my voice, "why did you accept this ride? You can barely keep your eyes open. You have missed three turns. You stopped for *face wash* and cigarettes. I am going to miss my flight."

He was quiet for a moment. The candy clicked against his teeth.

"Sir, I have been driving since 9 PM yesterday. This is my last ride. Airport means good fare. Long distance. If I don't take it, I go home with two hundred rupees for the night. Two hundred rupees." He held up two fingers without taking his hand off the wheel. "Morning I drive for school kids"

I sat back.

What do you say to that? What do you say to a man who has been awake for nine hours driving strangers through a city that was built by people like you, for people like you, and who has accepted your ride not because he is careless but because his daughter's school fees are due and two hundred rupees — less than two and a half dollars — is the difference between taking your ride and not taking it? What rhetoric do you deploy? What argument do you make? You are a twenty-three-year-old software engineer with a salary that would take him four years to earn, and you are angry because you might miss a flight, and he is angry at nothing because he learned long ago that anger at the structure of things is a luxury, like sleep, that he cannot afford.

I said nothing. We drove.

---

We reached the airport at 6:10 AM. I knew before I walked in. The building had that look — bright, efficient, sealed — the look of a system that does not wait. I ran anyway. Through the glass doors. Past the guards. To the check-in counter where a woman in a pressed uniform looked at my booking reference with the expression of a doctor examining an X-ray that confirms what she already suspected.

"Sir, check-in is closed."

"The flight is at 6:30. It's 6:12."

"Check-in closed at 6:00, sir. I'm sorry."

She was sorry. Everyone in the chain was sorry. The airline was sorry. The app was sorry. The algorithm that had matched me with a man who had been awake since yesterday evening was, presumably, sorry in whatever way algorithms are sorry — by adjusting a variable, by feeding the data point into a model that would, next time, pair a 6:30 AM airport ride with a driver who had slept.

I stood in the departures hall. Around me, the morning machinery of Indian air travel churned on — families with overpacked suitcases, businessmen in pressed shirts, a child asleep on her father's shoulder. The destination board flickered. Pune. 6:30. *Gate Closed.*

I booked another flight. 2:40 PM. Six hours away. I found a metal bench near a coffee stand and sat down. My mother would call soon. My sister would ask. I would explain, and the explanation would sound, as all explanations of delay sound to the people waiting for you, like an excuse.

I opened my phone. I looked at the ride. Forty-one kilometres. Ninety minutes. I thought about filing a complaint. My thumb hovered over the button the way a judge's hand hovers over a gavel — with the authority to act and the sudden, unwelcome awareness of what the act means. *Driver was drowsy. Missed turns. Made unscheduled stops. I missed my flight.*

All of it true. None of it untrue.

But behind the complaint, I saw him — the cabby from Hoskote, a town I had driven through a dozen times without stopping, a man with no degree and a tea stall near a toll plaza and a daughter whose school fees were due on Monday. A man who had been awake all night carrying strangers through a city that everyone in the world knows as India's technology capital but which is, at four in the morning, at road level, just a dark maze of broken asphalt and unfinished construction where a man drives in circles to earn enough to keep his life from falling apart, playing loud music not because he likes it but because silence, for him, means sleep, and sleep means no fare, and no fare means Monday comes and he has nothing.

I put the phone down.

The coffee was bad. The bench was cold. Outside, the sun was coming up over Bengaluru, painting the glass towers gold and leaving the roads beneath them in shadow, as it does every morning, as it has always done — illuminating the top of things and leaving the machinery underneath in darkness, where it hums and turns and carries the whole shining apparatus on its back without anyone flying out of this airport at 6:30 AM ever needing to know how, or at what cost, or who was awake all night to make it possible.

My sister texted at eight.

"Where are you," she said. Not a question.

No question mark. She has already understood something it took me twenty-three years to learn — that the question mark is a symbol of hope, and there are sentences that have given up on it.

I told her the whole thing.

Three dots appeared. Disappeared. Appeared again.

Then:

*"You have some genuinely dangerous problems in life."*

I stared at the screen. Then I laughed — alone, on a metal bench in an airport terminal, holding cold coffee, surrounded by strangers who were all going somewhere and arriving on time. A real laugh. The kind that comes not from humour but from recognition. From the sudden, unwelcome clarity that arrives when a seventeen-year-old girl, sitting in your mother's house in Pune, holding the phone you bought her, dismantles your entire experience with one sentence.

*Dangerous problems.* A cab driver who plays loud music. A missed turn on the way to an airport. A flight you can rebook with a few taps on a screen that costs more than the driver earns in a month. These are my dangerous problems. These are the emergencies of my life — a life in which the worst thing that can happen on a Saturday morning is that I arrive home six hours later than planned, to a house where lunch will be waiting regardless, made by a woman who will open the door and say nothing about the delay because she has already, in the hours between my missed flight and my arrival, converted her disappointment into *dal*.

Somewhere on the road back to Hoskote, the guy was driving home. Or maybe he wasn't driving home. Maybe he had picked up another fare — another engineer, another airport, another forty-one kilometres of staying awake because Monday was coming and Monday does not care how tired you are. His dangerous problems were not missing flights. His dangerous problems were the kind that don't make for funny texts to younger sisters. The kind you don't tell anyone about because telling requires an audience that has the luxury of listening, and the people in his life are too busy having the same problems to sit down and hear about his.

I drank the cold coffee. It tasted the way airport coffee always tastes — like waiting, like transit, like the particular flavour of a life lived in the space between the place you chose to be and the people who didn't choose to miss you but do.

Six hours. Then home.

---

*For the cabby, and for every man who has ever driven through the night so that the morning could work for someone else.*
